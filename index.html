Mission

The Davidow Project’s mission is to utilize Bluetooth Low Energy technology in order to interact with the Davidow Collection of artwork in the Arts and Technology building at The University of Texas at Dallas. Our app is currently under development, check here for updates on our progress…

Overview

The personal collection of Dallas collector and educator Joan Davidow are showcased throughout the Edith O’Donnell Arts and Technology building. Typically faculty, students, and administrators glance at the pieces as they go about their day. Our project provides the user a higher level of engagement with the collection as well as the ability to locate other pieces of interest.

Craft

An interactive app was developed to assist viewers of the Davidow collection in navigating the large gallery spaced throughout the ATEC building. The code was built from scratch using a combination of HTML, CSS and several other frameworks to deliver an interactive experience that would be fluid and responsive. The concept was to create a design that would represent the sophistication and detailed nature of the Davidow Collection. Ultimately the inclusion of estimotes, which send information about the art to a viewers phone, were used to make the experience one that fits in with the pioneering nature of ATEC students.

Process

What is BLE?

BLEs, Bluetooth Low Energy devices, allow devices to communicate bits of information between each other wirelessly. There are two parts required for our app to work. First, we send out a signal. Using a series of Estimote beacons, small rock-shaped devices that send out packets of information every few moments with a Bluetooth signal. Each beacon has its own unique ID, so we gave each piece of art its own beacon. We could then say, “This piece of art is associated with beacon number three, so anytime a phone hears beacon number three, it must be near that piece.” All that was left now was to build an app that would do the listening and display the relevant information.

Phase 1

The first stage of the Davidow Project involved creating a proof of concept app to show what was possible with BLE technology. Lance King, a graduate of the EMAC program at UTD, kicked off the Davidow Project by creating an app to map pieces of art depending on which beacons were detected. He encountered several obstacles along the way, such as determining with sufficient accuracy the distance from a beacon when there was an abundance of signal noise. His work laid the foundation for another group of developers and designers to build upon. In the spring of 2015, an Internet Studio II class began working on the Davidow Project alongside Lance, working together to bring his idea to life on a much larger scale.

Phase 2: Internet Studio 2

During phase two, students were divided into 3 teams and began to frame the Davidow design. The Research Team studied the artist’s work, conducted user interviews, and developed user evolution charts. This information gave the team a better understanding of how the user could be motivated to explore the Davidow app in its entirety.

The User Interface Team helped to design not only the visual appearance of the app, but also the functionality. Red Route diagrams, a list of design principles, and a UI kit were created to begin fleshing out what the application might look like. With the inclusion of a splash screen, an engaging onboarding session, and the website you’re exploring now, the UI team was able to produce the first look at how the app might appear on a smartphone.

The Development Team worked to define the beacon functionality and how to pair it with the artwork in the ATEC building. In addition, the developers worked with the other teams to tie all the pieces together in order to create a solid foundation for the continuation of the Davidow Project.

Phase 3: Lean Mean Team

Phase 3 is the last stage of the Davidow Project and consists of the lean, mean team – Judy Nguyen, Dylan Orgeron, Dylan Carroll, and Summer Saylor. Starting in the Fall 2015 semester, 4 students and 1 professor embarked on a journey to further refine and complete the Davidow website and app, and ultimately launch the app into the Google Play store.

The team began the semester by outlining their goals and researching how BLE devices and other similar technologies, like RFID and NFC tags, were being used. After exploring various academic journals and online articles, the team found several innovative ways to use bluetooth and NFC tags that helped inspire new features of the app.

Once the team learned a little bit about how other people were using this type of technology, they began their work on the Davidow app and website. You can see their progress here on their Github page.
